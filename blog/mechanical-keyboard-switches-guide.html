Mechanical Keyboard Switches Guide for Gaming: The Complete Breakdown of Every Switch Type

The mechanical keyboard switches guide everyone seems to write is full of vague claims and stock photos. This one is different. We're going to break down exactly how linear, tactile, and clicky switches work at a physical level — and which one will genuinely help your in-game performance in 2025.

What is a Mechanical Keyboard Switch, Actually?

A mechanical keyboard switch is a self-contained electromechanical unit that sits beneath each keycap. When you press a key, the switch stem travels down a housing, compresses a spring, makes electrical contact, and sends a signal to your computer. The entire travel is typically 4mm, and the signal is registered somewhere between 1.5mm and 2.5mm down — this point is called the actuation point.

What makes mechanical switches different from membrane keyboards is that the actuation is controlled by physical mechanics — spring tension, stem shape, stem material — rather than a rubber membrane pressing against a circuit. This means more consistency, longer lifespan (typically 50–100 million keystrokes), and faster reset distance for rapid re-presses.

Linear Switches: The FPS Gamer's Default

Linear switches have a completely smooth travel from top to bottom. No bump. No click. Just a straight, consistent glide. The force required increases gradually as you press deeper, governed purely by spring compression.

Key specs for gaming: Cherry MX Red — 45g actuation, 2mm actuation point, 4mm total travel. Gateron Red — 35–45g actuation, slightly smoother than Cherry at a lower price point.

Why gamers love linear switches

The absence of a tactile bump means less resistance on rapid key presses. In games where you're spam-pressing WASD for movement — CS2 counter-strafing, Apex bunny hopping, Valorant movement tech — a lighter linear switch reduces the force required per keystroke and allows faster reset. Over a 3-hour session, this matters.

Linear switches are the overwhelming choice among top CS2 and Valorant pros. Cherry MX Red and Gateron Yellow (45g, ultra-smooth) are the most commonly spotted switches in pro setups.

Downsides of linear switches

Without tactile or audible feedback, it's easy to bottom out — pressing the key all the way to the housing. This is slightly slower than stopping at the actuation point. Beginners especially tend to bottom out constantly on linears. O-ring dampeners can help, but they add a small amount of travel reduction and bounce-back variation.

Tactile Switches: Precision Typing Feel, Competitive Feel

Tactile switches have a noticeable bump at the actuation point. When your finger reaches the actuation depth, you feel a clear physical resistance bump — feedback that the keypress has registered. No click, just feel.

Key specs: Cherry MX Brown — 45g actuation, 2mm actuation, tactile bump at 2mm. Glorious Pandas — 67g, pronounced tactile bump, beloved in the typing community.

When tactile switches shine for gaming

Tactile switches give you confirmation without noise. In strategy games, MOBAs, and MMOs where you need to time abilities precisely, the tactile bump gives you accuracy feedback. Some FPS players also prefer tactile — particularly those who type extensively alongside gaming and don't want to switch keyboards.

Note: Cherry MX Browns are widely considered to have a "scratchy" feel compared to Gateron Browns, which are smoother. If you want tactile, Gateron Browns or Topre-style switches are generally considered more premium for the money.

Clicky Switches: Satisfying, But Know the Trade-Offs

Clicky switches combine a tactile bump with an audible click at the actuation point. The click comes from a small plastic click jacket that snaps against the housing stem as the key is pressed.

Key specs: Cherry MX Blue — 50g actuation, 2mm actuation, 2.2mm reset point (note: reset is above actuation — faster release needed for rapid re-presses). Razer Green — similar to Cherry MX Blue. Gateron Blue — slightly smoother travel than Cherry.

The gaming problem with clicky switches

The Cherry MX Blue's reset point (2.2mm) is above its actuation point (2mm). This means you must release the key more than 2.2mm before you can register a second press. For rapid-fire inputs like bunny hopping in CS2 or rapid jiggle-peeking, this gap can cause missed keypresses that cost rounds. This is the primary reason competitive FPS players tend to avoid clicky switches.

The noise is also a social and environmental factor — clicky switches in headset-equipped competitive environments are audible to teammates on voice comms.

For competitive FPS specifically, the recommendation is clear: go linear. Lighter linear switches (35–45g actuation) allow faster movement tech execution. Heavier linears (55–67g) reduce accidental keystrokes but require more effort per keypress.

Popular Switch Brands Compared

Cherry MX

The original standard. Cherry MX Reds are reliable and widely available. The downside is they're not the smoothest on the market anymore, and they're priced at a premium despite being outclassed by competitors in feel.

Gateron

Gateron switches are widely considered smoother than Cherry at a lower price. Gateron Yellows (45g linear) are a favorite among competitive players for their factory lubed, ultra-smooth keystroke. Outstanding value.

Razer

Razer's mechanical switches (Green = clicky, Orange = tactile, Yellow = linear) are reliable and well-suited to Razer's own keyboards. The Razer Yellow is an excellent linear switch for FPS gaming.

SteelSeries OmniPoint / Magnetic Hall Effect

SteelSeries has moved into analog Hall Effect switches (OmniPoint) which use magnetic fields instead of physical contact. They offer adjustable actuation points from 0.2mm to 3.8mm — useful for players who want to customize their response curve. No physical wear, essentially infinite lifespan.
