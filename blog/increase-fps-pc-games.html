How to Increase FPS in Modern PC Games: 15 Tweaks That Actually Work

More FPS isn't just bragging rights — it's a competitive edge at every refresh rate tier. These 15 tweaks are ordered by impact and cover in-game settings, GPU drivers, Windows optimization, and hardware-level changes that can realistically gain you 30–50% more frames.

Step 0: Identify Your Bottleneck First

Before tweaking anything, you need to know whether your system is GPU-limited or CPU-limited in the games you play. Use your GPU monitoring overlay (MSI Afterburner + RivaTuner, or NVIDIA FrameView) during gameplay and check GPU utilization. If GPU utilization is consistently above 95%, you're GPU-limited — focus on tweaks 1–7. If GPU utilization is below 80% and your CPU is maxed, you're CPU-limited — skip to tweaks 8–15.

Use our Frame Time Calculator to understand the relationship between your current FPS and your monitor's Hz — this tells you how large the gap is between what you have and what your display can use.

MSI Afterburner overlay: GPU at 97% = GPU bottleneck. CPU at 90% with GPU at 60% = CPU bottleneck. Different fixes required.

In-Game Settings: The Highest-Impact Tweaks

  1. Lower Render Scale / Resolution Scale to 90–95%. Imperceptible quality difference, 10–20% FPS gain. Most important tweak if GPU-limited.
  2. Disable Ray Tracing and DLSS Quality → DLSS Performance. Ray tracing is the single most GPU-expensive feature in modern games.
  3. Set Shadow Quality to Medium. Shadows are GPU-intensive and the quality reduction from High to Medium is almost invisible during fast-paced play.
  4. Disable Ambient Occlusion. Another high-cost effect with minimal competitive visibility benefit.
  5. Enable DLSS, FSR, or XeSS in supported games. These AI upscalers provide near-native image quality at significantly reduced rendering costs.

GPU Driver Tweaks

  1. Update GPU drivers. New drivers frequently include game-specific optimizations that improve FPS in specific titles by 5–15%.
  2. Set Texture Filtering Quality to Performance in NVIDIA Control Panel. Minimal visual impact, measurable FPS gain.
  3. Enable Resizable BAR (ReBAR). If your CPU and motherboard support it (Intel 10th gen+ / AMD Ryzen 3000+), enabling ReBAR in BIOS can improve FPS in modern titles by 5–12%.

These in-game settings have the highest FPS impact relative to visual quality loss in competitive games.

System-Level Tweaks

  1. Enable XMP/EXPO for your RAM in BIOS. Running DDR4/DDR5 at rated speeds (not the default 2133MHz JEDEC) can increase FPS significantly in CPU-limited games — sometimes 15–20% in titles like CS2.
  2. Close background applications. Chrome tabs, Discord video, stream software all consume VRAM and CPU cycles. Use Task Manager to identify and close high-CPU background processes before sessions.
  3. Set Windows power plan to High Performance or Ultimate Performance. See our input lag guide for the exact steps.
  4. Ensure your game is set to run on your discrete GPU. Settings → System → Display → Graphics → add your game executable → set to High Performance.
  5. Defragment your HDD (not SSD) if the game is installed on spinning disk. Fragmented game files cause hitches and stutter during level loading that tank frame time consistency.
  6. Clean your GPU cooler. Thermal throttling from dust-clogged heatsinks can drop GPU clock speeds by 15–30%, directly reducing FPS. Compressed air clean every 6 months.
  7. Reinstall the game to an NVMe SSD. Shader compilation and texture streaming stutter is dramatically reduced on NVMe vs SATA SSD vs HDD.
