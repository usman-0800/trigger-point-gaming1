What is eDPI and How to Calculate It? The Only Number That Really Matters for FPS Sensitivity

eDPI — Effective DPI — is the single number that actually defines your mouse sensitivity, and understanding what eDPI is and how to calculate it is essential if you want to compare settings, copy pro configs, or switch games without destroying your muscle memory. This guide explains everything from the ground up.

What is eDPI, Exactly?

eDPI stands for Effective DPI. It's a calculated number that represents your true mouse sensitivity by combining two variables: your mouse's hardware DPI setting and your in-game sensitivity multiplier. The formula is brutally simple:

eDPI = Mouse DPI × In-Game Sensitivity

So if you run 800 DPI at 0.4 in-game sensitivity in Valorant, your eDPI is 320. If your teammate runs 400 DPI at 0.8, their eDPI is also 320. Different numbers on paper — identical feel in the game. That's the power of eDPI: it strips away the hardware vs. software confusion and gives you one clean, comparable number.

This is why eDPI is the standard unit of communication in the competitive FPS community. When a pro player streams and says "I'm on 400 eDPI," you know exactly how sensitive their mouse is, regardless of what DPI or in-game setting they chose to achieve that.

How to Calculate eDPI — Step by Step

Calculating eDPI manually takes about five seconds. But our free eDPI calculator does it in one — just enter your DPI and in-game sensitivity and it spits out your eDPI plus a sensitivity category label and a visual gauge.

Here's the manual method:

  1. Find your mouse DPI: Check your mouse software (Logitech G HUB, Razer Synapse, SteelSeries GG) or look at your mouse's physical DPI button label. Common values: 400, 800, 1600.
  2. Find your in-game sensitivity: Open your game settings. In CS2 it's under Game → Mouse Settings. In Valorant it's under Settings → General.
  3. Multiply them: eDPI = DPI × sensitivity.

What is the Ideal eDPI Range for Competitive Gaming?

The "ideal" eDPI is deeply personal and depends on your game, grip style, and physical setup. However, the competitive community has established some useful ranges based on what the majority of pro players converge around.

For CS2 (Counter-Strike 2): Most pro players fall between 700 and 1200 eDPI. The average hovers around 900. This translates to roughly 35–55 cm of physical mouse movement for a full 360-degree turn.

For Valorant: Because Valorant uses a much higher sensitivity multiplier internally (its yaw value is 0.07, versus CS2's 0.022), the in-game numbers look much smaller. Pros typically run 200–400 eDPI. Don't be alarmed — this is the same physical feel as a mid-range CS2 sensitivity.

For Apex Legends: Typical pro eDPI is 1200–2000. Apex is a faster-paced game where quicker mouse movements pay dividends.

General guidance: Below 400 eDPI is considered low/arm aim. 400–1000 is medium. Above 1000 is high/wrist aim. Most beginners start too high — high sensitivity masks imprecision, which feels like faster reactions but actually hurts long-term aim development.

Why eDPI Beats Just Talking About "Sensitivity"

Here's a common confusion: two players are both running CS2. Player A says they use "sensitivity 2.0." Player B says they use "sensitivity 0.9." You'd assume Player A is playing on much higher sensitivity, right? But if Player A has a 400 DPI mouse and Player B has a 1600 DPI mouse, they're actually playing at 800 eDPI and 1440 eDPI respectively. Player B is on much higher effective sensitivity despite the lower in-game number.

This is why raw in-game sensitivity numbers are meaningless without the DPI context. eDPI solves this. It's the apples-to-apples comparison that makes every sensitivity discussion actually useful.

How to Actually Use eDPI to Improve Your Aim

Knowing your eDPI is the first step. Here's how to put it to work:

Step 1: Calculate and assess your current eDPI

Use our eDPI calculator. If you're above 2000 eDPI in CS2 or above 600 in Valorant, you're almost certainly at a disadvantage in precision — consider stepping down gradually.

Step 2: Find your target range

Pick a target eDPI based on the ranges above for your game. If you don't know where to start, aim for the median pro value.

Step 3: Decide your DPI/sensitivity split

For best sensor accuracy, keep hardware DPI between 400 and 1600. Adjust in-game sensitivity to hit your target eDPI. Avoid running DPI above 3200 — interpolation at high DPI can reduce sensor accuracy.

Step 4: Commit and build muscle memory

Give yourself at least three to four weeks. Your eDPI number means nothing if you don't give your hands enough time to learn it. Consistency beats optimization every time.
